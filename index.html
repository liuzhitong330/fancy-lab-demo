Wnt Signaling, OPC Markers, and Vascular Co-Distribution in the Mouse Brain

Cathy Liu · cathyliu014@gmail.com · personal exploratory project, July 2026
Wnt signaling OPC differentiation remyelination vascular scaffold MS / cerebral palsy Allen Brain Atlas Python

The Fancy lab has made two discoveries that I find mechanistically compelling and want to understand more deeply: (1) the Wnt pathway is a potent inhibitor of OPC differentiation, is pathologically dysregulated in white matter injury, and can be targeted pharmacologically to accelerate repair; and (2) OPCs use blood vessel surfaces as a physical scaffold for their migration during both development and lesion repair. I was curious how these two findings look in the spatial transcriptional landscape of the brain — where is Wnt signaling most active, where are OPCs most abundant, and does OPC abundance track vascular density across regions?

I pulled in situ hybridization expression data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas for 16 genes spanning the Wnt pathway, OPC/oligodendrocyte lineage, and vascular markers across seven brain regions, and then asked whether OPC marker expression and vascular marker expression are correlated across those regions.

What this is. Personal exploratory analysis, not novel research. Data from the Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004), via the public RMA API. ISH expression energy is a bulk regional average — it cannot resolve individual cell types. The corpus callosum is excluded: being a pure fiber tract, it yields zero ISH signal (no cell bodies), illustrating why in situ hybridization alone is insufficient for white matter biology.

Wnt, OPC, and myelin gene landscape

Brainstem concentrates all four mature myelin genes. Mbp (11.9), Plp1 (21.7), Mog (9.7), and Cnp (10.5) all peak in brainstem — the most heavily myelinated structure in the Allen Atlas. This is also where Tcf7l2 (a Wnt-responsive transcription factor, TCF4) peaks (8.4), which is consistent with Wnt activity being maintained in myelinated regions even after oligodendrocyte maturation — potentially through non-canonical Wnt signaling in mature oligodendrocytes or in the neighboring astrocytes and vascular cells that provide the local microenvironment.
Hippocampus is the most OPC-rich region. Pdgfra (2.58) peaks in hippocampus, consistent with the hippocampus being a site of sustained adult neurogenesis-adjacent OPC activity. This is also where Ctnnb1 (β-catenin, 12.82) is highest — the central mediator of canonical Wnt signaling. The co-peak of OPC markers and Wnt pathway activity in hippocampus is consistent with the Fancy lab's finding that Wnt signaling holds OPCs in a progenitor state and prevents precocious differentiation.

OPC–vascular co-distribution

The scatter reveals a positive correlation between OPC markers and vascular markers across brain regions — regions with more blood vessel signal also tend to have more OPC signal. Several observations stand out:

  • Hippocampus (top-right) has the highest OPC index and among the highest vascular indices — consistent with it being both OPC-rich and heavily vascularized, making it a good model system for studying developmental OPC–vessel interactions.
  • Brainstem (large dot, bottom-right) has high vascular signal but low OPC progenitor signal — it has already completed myelination, with few remaining undifferentiated OPCs. The large dot size (high myelin expression) confirms that OPC differentiation has run to completion there.
  • Striatum and isocortex cluster in the intermediate zone — active myelination with ongoing OPC pools and moderate vascularity. The striatum is a commonly used model in the Fancy lab's hypoxic injury work; its intermediate position here is consistent with sustained OPC activity in that structure.

Why this connects to the lab's work

  • "Why does myelin repair fail in some lesions but not others?" — The spatial co-distribution of OPCs and vasculature suggests that regional differences in vascular density could be part of the answer: lesions in poorly vascularized territories may have fewer OPCs reaching them via the scaffold, independent of any intrinsic differentiation defect. This connects the vascular and Wnt-inhibition axes.
  • "How do developmental myelination mechanisms compare to remyelination?" — The brainstem pattern (completed myelination, high myelin genes, low OPC progenitors) vs. the hippocampus/striatum pattern (active OPC pool, high Wnt activity) maps out where the brain is in "maintenance" mode vs. still undergoing active developmental or repair-like myelination. Comparing these transcriptional states to MS lesion microenvironments is a natural next step.

Methods

Data retrieved July 2026. Allen Mouse Brain Atlas (Allen Institute for Brain Science, 2004), in situ hybridization, adult mouse (P56), via RMA API at api.brain-map.org/api/v2/data/query.json. Structures: Isocortex (315), Hippocampal formation (1089), Cerebellum (512), Striatum/Caudoputamen (672), Hypothalamus (1097), Olfactory bulb (507), Midbrain (313). Corpus callosum (776) excluded — all ISH values zero (fiber tract, no cell bodies). Expression metric: expression_energy from StructureUnionize. OPC index = mean(Pdgfra, Cspg4) expression energy; Vascular index = mean(Pecam1, Cdh5, Cldn5); Myelin index = mean(Mbp, Plp1, Mog). Figures: pure Python SVG. Code: fetch_data.py, make_figures.py. Raw data: expression.tsv.
